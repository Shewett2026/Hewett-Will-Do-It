Practical Ideas for Bakersfield

Not talking points. Real proposals that deserve a hard look.

  • “Most people don’t distrust government because they hate it. They distrust what they can’t understand.”

    Most city budgets are technically public: buried in PDFs, spreadsheets, and dense documents that require a finance background to interpret. That’s not transparency. That’s paperwork with a window.

    An AI-powered budget portal would let any resident ask plain-language questions (where does my money go, what does the city spend on roads in Ward 3, how much of Measure N goes to police versus other services) and get real answers, linked to real data.

    Cities like Fort Lauderdale, Rancho Palos Verdes, and San Bernardino already use interactive budget tools. Bakersfield can build on that. And go further.

    City Hall should be understandable. Full stop.

  • “Working inside Bakersfield Fire, I’ve seen how fast incidents scale. And how much faster neighbors can respond than any unit on the road.”

    Bakersfield used to train residents to assist during emergencies. That program faded. It shouldn’t have.

    A modern Citizen Emergency Preparedness Program would cover CPR, basic first aid, disaster evacuation routes, communication with 911 under pressure, how to respond to downed power lines, and how to help neighbors before first responders arrive.

    Our region is vulnerable to earthquakes, extreme heat events, flooding, and wildfires. A trained community is a more resilient one.

    The City of Los Angeles, Tulsa, and San Jose all run active community emergency response programs. Bakersfield had one. We can restart it better.

    Don’t wait for a disaster to wish we had prepared.

  • “Your voice shouldn’t require taking time off work.”

    City Council meetings start at 5:30 PM. That’s not accessible for most working parents, shift workers, elderly residents, or anyone without transportation to City Hall.

    A vetted video comment system would allow residents to submit recorded public comments before meetings. Each comment is reviewed for eligibility, then played during the public comment period. The voice of Ward 3 shouldn’t belong only to people who can make it downtown on a Tuesday evening.

    Vetting protects the process. Access expands participation.

    If we want more people engaged, we have to meet them where they are.

  • Every permit applicant deserves clear expectations instead of endless waiting.

    Within 24 Hours: Your application is received, reviewed for completeness, and assigned to the appropriate department. If anything is missing, you’ll know exactly what needs to be fixed.

    Within 48 Hours: The City provides an initial review, identifies any major issues, and gives you a clear roadmap of the remaining approval process instead of leaving you guessing.

    Within 60 Days: Final approval, denial, or a written explanation for why additional time is legally required. No permit should disappear into a bureaucratic black hole.

    Every applicant should be able to track their permit’s status online, know which department currently has it, and receive automatic updates as it moves through the process.

    Clear timelines. Transparent progress. Accountable government.

    Bakersfield should become one of the easiest cities in California to build, rebuild, and invest in.

  • “Some parts of Bakersfield feel designed to bake people alive.”

    Northeast Bakersfield has some of the lowest tree canopy coverage in the city. That means hotter sidewalks, hotter bus stops, hotter walks home from school. Kids, elderly residents, and working families feel that directly.

    Strategic tree planting along corridors and near bus stops is not landscaping. It is public health infrastructure. Urban tree canopy can reduce local surface temperatures by 5 to 10 degrees Fahrenheit. Cottonwood trees, native to the Kern Valley, grow fast and are well-suited to our climate.

    This work can be funded primarily through state and federal infrastructure and environmental grants. Not new local taxes.

    Shade is not a luxury. In Bakersfield summers, it is a safety issue.

  • “Not everyone experiencing homelessness needs the same solution. Our response shouldn’t treat them as if they do.”

    People experiencing homelessness often have very different circumstances, yet they’re frequently placed into the same system. A veteran between jobs, a young adult aging out of foster care, a parent with children, someone recovering from addiction, and someone living with severe mental illness all require different paths forward.

    Those with the greatest likelihood of returning to independent living should receive priority access to our best existing housing resources, employment assistance, and transition programs. By investing where stability is most achievable, we can help more people successfully rebuild their lives.

    For individuals facing severe mental illness, substance dependency, or other conditions that require intensive daily support, Bakersfield should pursue secure service campuses designed specifically for recovery and stabilization. These campuses would provide comprehensive care in an environment built for treatment, safety, and long-term success. As residents recover and become ready for greater independence, they can transition into existing housing programs within the community.

    These campuses are not intended to resemble institutions or correctional facilities. They are designed to restore dignity while providing the structure and services many people need to move forward.

    A Better Model of Care

    Instead of spending hundreds of thousands of dollars on a single “affordable housing” unit, organizations such as the Everdawn Foundation have demonstrated that high-quality transitional units can be built for a fraction of the cost.

    Each resident should have access to the fundamentals of stability:

    • A private, lockable living unit
    • A personal mailbox for identification and government correspondence
    • A bed, toilet, and secure storage for personal belongings
    • Daily meals and shower facilities
    • Private meeting space for social workers, counselors, and healthcare providers
    • The ability to keep companion animals when appropriate
    • Small personal gardens that encourage routine, ownership, and responsibility
    • Gated campuses that provide a safe environment for residents, staff, and visitors

    These are places for recovery and rebuilding. Not jail cells. Not sidewalks. Not million-dollar housing units.

    We can provide compassionate care, improve public safety, and help more people successfully return to independent living by matching resources to the needs of the individual instead of relying on a one-size-fits-all approach.

  • “Companies looking to expand inland want to know the lights will stay on. Right now, that answer is complicated.”

    As Bakersfield grows, attracting distribution centers, data warehouses, and agricultural technology companies, grid reliability becomes an economic development issue, not just an infrastructure one.

    At the city level, I want to start the conversation about energy resilience: supporting state and federal investments in advanced energy infrastructure, advocating for Bakersfield’s inclusion in emerging energy pilot programs, and ensuring that our growth trajectory isn’t limited by an aging grid.

    Small Modular Reactors are part of a serious national conversation about energy independence. Bakersfield’s energy expertise and industrial infrastructure make this region a logical partner.

    This is a long game. The right time to start it is now.

Where I Stand

Because leadership means being clear with people.

  • Measure N should focus on what only government can do.

    Firefighters, police officers, code enforcement officers, and School Resource Officers carry the responsibility of keeping Bakersfield safe every single day. Yet Measure N funding has gradually expanded across so many priorities that its original purpose has become less focused.

    I believe it’s time to return Measure N to its core mission.

    My priority is increasing funding for Police, Fire, Code Enforcement, and School Resource Officers by improving staffing, replacing aging equipment, strengthening recruitment and retention, and ensuring these departments have the resources they need to meet the demands of a growing city.

    When Measure N dollars are invested outside these core public safety services, taxpayers deserve measurable results. Every program receiving public safety funding should meet clear performance standards and publicly report whether it is reducing crime, improving emergency response, or helping people transition out of homelessness.

    Public safety begins with trust. Trust begins with accountability.

    Measure N should fund results, not simply good intentions.

  • Nobody should fear summer inside their own home.

    Bakersfield summers are among the hottest in California. For many families, air conditioning is a comfort. For seniors living on fixed incomes, renters in aging apartments, and residents with medical conditions, it can be the difference between life and death.

    I support establishing reasonable minimum cooling standards for residential housing. Property owners should provide safe, functional cooling infrastructure, just as they provide plumbing, electricity, and safe construction.

    Bakersfield should never become a refuge for slumlords who allow elderly residents to suffer through dangerous indoor temperatures because basic cooling systems were neglected or never maintained.

    We can protect vulnerable residents while remaining fair to responsible property owners.

    Nobody should lose their life because their home became hotter than the street outside.

  • “My family helped secure the Kern River for Bakersfield. Water stewardship is not theoretical to me.”

    My great-grandfather, as Vice Mayor, helped negotiate the City’s acquisition of Kern River water rights from Tenneco West in the 1970s, a deal that was unpopular at the time and is now recognized as one of the most consequential civic decisions in Bakersfield’s history.

    That kind of long-term thinking is exactly what water policy requires. Our river, our groundwater, and our agricultural water supply are not abstract policy questions. They are the backbone of everything that grows here.

    I will advocate for consistent river flow, transparent water management, and interagency cooperation that puts Bakersfield’s long-term water security first. Not short-term convenience.

    We did not inherit this water to waste it.

  • If cannabis is already here, Bakersfield should benefit from it.

    Whether we like it or not, cannabis is already being sold throughout Bakersfield. Right now, illegal operators profit while taxpayers receive nothing in return. We lose millions in potential local revenue that could instead strengthen our community.

    I support creating a tightly regulated local cannabis market that directs tax revenue back into Bakersfield.

    Those dollars should help fund neighborhood beautification, public safety, parks, youth recreation, graffiti removal, code enforcement, and small business revitalization. My proposed Community Mural Grant Program is one example.

    Small grants could help local businesses commission murals celebrating Bakersfield’s history, agriculture, energy industry, veterans, and cultural heritage while transforming neglected walls into public art.

    Every dollar currently flowing into the illegal market is a dollar that could improve our neighborhoods instead.

    If the market already exists, Bakersfield should be the one benefiting from it.

  • Compassion should solve homelessness, not manage it.

    For decades, governments and nonprofit organizations across America have spent billions responding to homelessness, yet thousands of people remain on our streets.

    The problem is that homelessness has become one broad category instead of many distinct challenges.

    A homeless veteran is not facing the same barriers as someone suffering from severe addiction. A young adult aging out of foster care needs something different than a person with chronic mental illness. Families who simply lost housing should not be placed into the same system as repeat offenders with extensive criminal histories.

    We should stop treating every case the same.

    I support creating secure, low-cost housing campuses where people can safely live while receiving services that match their needs. Residents would have a bed, a locking door, a mailing address, showers, meals, medical care, case management, and opportunities to rebuild stability.

    For veterans, foster youth, seniors, and people facing temporary hardship, these communities should provide our very best resources to help people regain independence as quickly as possible.

    For individuals struggling with chronic addiction or extensive criminal histories, secure and humane campuses can provide treatment, structure, and accountability while reducing the burden on emergency rooms, firefighters, police officers, and county jails.

    Our first responders should not spend thousands of hours responding to the same preventable crises. Our businesses should not bear the economic cost of unmanaged encampments. Our neighborhoods deserve to feel safe and welcoming again.

    Most importantly, people experiencing homelessness deserve better than a tent and another referral.

    The goal should never be to manage homelessness. The goal should be to end it.

  • “Bakersfield is the next frontier for distribution, logistics, and advanced industry. That opportunity will not wait.”

    Companies are being priced out of coastal California. Bakersfield’s location, workforce, and land availability make us an obvious destination, if we make it easy to come here.

    That means a city that actively recruits investment, fast-tracks permits for new businesses, and ensures our energy and infrastructure can support growth. It also means protecting the businesses that are already here: the shops, restaurants, and services that make Ward 3 worth living in.

    I will be an active advocate for Bakersfield’s economic potential at every level.

    A city that grows its economy grows options for everyone who lives here.

  • “Punjabi is the third most spoken language in Bakersfield. Our schools have not caught up to that reality.”

    French is widely taught in local schools. Punjabi, spoken daily by a significant portion of our community, is almost entirely absent from the curriculum.

    I encourage the school districts within Ward 3’s jurisdiction to explore Punjabi language courses as a practical, community-relevant option for students. This is not a mandate. It is a recognition that language education should reflect the community it serves.

    A student who learns Punjabi gains a skill with real professional and cultural value in Bakersfield and the broader Central Valley.

    Teach what matters to the people who are actually here.
